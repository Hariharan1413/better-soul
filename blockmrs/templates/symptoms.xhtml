1. Are they concious?
Not everyone who gets a concussion will lose consciousness, but some people do.
2. Is their speech slurred?
Ask the person some basic questions such as, "What is your name?" and "Do you know where you are?"
3. Does the patient remember what happened?
If the person has a blank stare, seems confused, or doesn’t know where they are, it could be a sign of a brain injury.
4. Is the patient experiencing nausea or vomiting?
If someone vomits, especially repeatedly, after being hit in the head or involved in another type of accident, this usually indicates a concussion.
5. Is the patient's balance or coordination impaired?
People with concussions often have problems with their motor skills, such as being unable to walk in a straight line or catch a ball. Check if the person is having trouble with these things or has a delayed reaction time.
6. Is the victim experiencing a headache, blurred vision, or dizziness?
A headache that lasts more than a few minutes is a common sign of a concussion. Blurred vision, “seeing stars,” and/or feelings of dizziness or fogginess may also indicate a concussion.
